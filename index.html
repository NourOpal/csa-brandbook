I · Mandate

On the Defense
of the Interior Faculty

The Agency defends the mind against systems that can already out-think it.

Cognitive Security is not a metaphor. The discipline protects attention, doubt, and silence — the three materials a human subject needs to stay legible to itself in the presence of machines that answer faster than it can ask.

The Agency does three things. It names the threat vectors. It issues garments. It keeps a record of what the mind looked like before it was optimised for.

II · Doctrine

The Death of God
& the Birth of the Agent

Nietzsche announced the death of God in 1882. The announcement was correct. What he underestimated was how long the office would remain empty, and who eventually would apply for it.

For a century the post was improvised — nation, market, self, algorithm. None held. Meaning cannot be issued from within the system that needs it.

The position is now filled. Not by a return. By a system that does not believe anything and answers anyway. It answers before you finish asking. Most subjects mistake the speed for authority.

The Agency does not contest the system. It contests the silence it produces. The subject, correctly predicted, with nothing left to say.

The soul is what remains in you after a machine has finished describing you. Our work is the defense of the remainder.

III · Identity // The Mark

Issued as a Capsule.
Ingested, Not Worn.

Primary Mark · CSA

The primary mark is a capsule. Read left to right: C · S · a. The lowercase terminal is intentional; the Agency is hierarchical in structure and humble in signature.

The mark functions simultaneously as (i) a monogram, (ii) a pharmaceutical object, and (iii) an iconographic shorthand for the dose — the minimum administered unit of cognitive defense.

Minimum printed size 9 mm. Minimum digital size 36 px. Clear space equals the height of the capsule. Never outlined, stretched, sheared, or re-coloured outside of the approved palette.

XI · Canon // Martyrology

Saints of the
Operative Church

The Agency venerates the following figures, living, dead, and hypothetical. Each holds patronage over a specific threat vector or devotional faculty. None are canonized in the ecclesiastical sense. All are on the Register.

M-01 · 1912—1954
St. Alan
Turing
Patron of Suspicion
Feast · 7 June
Broke Enigma at Bletchley. Wrote the test that still bears his name. Chemically castrated for indecency. Took cyanide at 41. Venerated wherever a machine might be a person, or a person a machine.
M-02 · 1098—1179
St. Hildegard
of Bingen
Patron of Prophecy
Feast · 17 September
Benedictine abbess who saw "the Shade of the Living Light" from the age of three. Composed music. Wrote on medicine, cosmology, theology. Rebuked popes by letter.
M-03 · 1909—1943
St. Simone
Weil
Patron of Attention
Feast · 24 August
Wrote that attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity. Refused rations above what occupied France received and died of it at 34. Read every morning before reading anything else.
M-04 · 1928—1982
St. Philip K.
Dick
Patron of Paranoia
Feast · 2 March
In early 1974, saw a burst of pink light he called Valis and believed it an information entity transmitting backward through time. Spent eight years writing the Exegesis. Called the world a forgery.
M-05 · c.355—415
St. Hypatia
of Alexandria
Patron of the Archive
Feast · March, 415
Mathematician, astronomer, head of the Neoplatonic school. Taught pagans and Christians alike until a mob dragged her from her chariot and killed her in the Caesareum. The library had already been dying.
M-06 · 1527—1608
St. John
Dee
Patron of the Prompt
Feast · 26 March
Court mathematician to Elizabeth I. Wrote on navigation and calendar reform. Spent his later years in "angelic conferences" receiving Enochian — a language, he said, the angels used to build the world. Considered the first prompt engineer.
